Ocean Acidification

What Causes Ocean Acidification?

Each year 14 to 15 billion tons of fossil fuels like coal, oil, and natural gases are burned. This produces over 37 billion tons of carbon dioxide emissions! The ocean acts as a giant sponge absorbing over 30% of these carbon dioxide gases. When the carbon dioxide and seawater mix, it triggers a chemical reaction. This causes the acidic level to increase in the water. So let’s be real, the real cause of ocean acidification is us, human beings.

Statistics

Since the Industrial Revolution, pH has dropped from 8.2 to 8.1, which is about a 25 to 30% increase in acidity. Due to human carbon emissions, which are increasing exponentially, it is projected that by 2100, pH will drop even more to around 7.7 or 7.8, which represents a 150% increase in acidity, something that hasn’t been seen for millions of years. Ocean acidification is happening at a rate that is 10 times faster than in the previous 300 million years. As for carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, in 2013, it passed 400 parts per million, which is the highest in the last 25 million years. The safe level of carbon dioxide is around 350 ppm, but we already reached that level back in 1988. Without the oceans absorbing excess carbon dioxide, climate change and global warming would be happening at a much faster rate, and carbon dioxide in the atmosphere would be much higher, close to 475 ppm.

How Does It Affect Humans?

Ocean acidification affects humans by disrupting the coastal economies. As the acid level in the ocean rises, it severely damages calcifying organisms. Calcifying organisms are anything that builds protective shells made out of calcium carbonate. These include clams, oysters, corals, etc. This decline in marine life directly threatens the livelihood of many people who depend on fishing and tourism, especially in coastal communities.

Who Is Affected by Ocean Acidification?

  • Humans
  • Coral Reefs
  • Fish
  • Plankton
  • Almost All Marine Organisms
